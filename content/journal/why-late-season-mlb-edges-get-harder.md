# Why late-season MLB edges get harder, not easier.

The intuition is wrong. Most bettors assume that as the season progresses and more data accumulates, the model gets sharper and the edges get bigger. The data piece is true. The edge piece is not.

By August, the MLB market has digested 100 games of evidence on every team. The opening lines reflect that evidence. The closing lines reflect even more. The cheap edges that existed in May, when the market was still anchored on preseason projections, are gone. What remains is harder.

## The market sharpens with you.

A model improves with more data. So does the market. The same information that lets the model price the season's third Yankees-Red Sox matchup more accurately also lets the books price it more accurately. The race between model and market is not a one-sided improvement.

In the first six weeks of the season, model and market are both working from limited samples. The market sometimes gets badly anchored on the wrong narrative. Edges open up. By August, both sides are working from full samples and the gap closes.

This is not a flaw in the model. It is the market doing its job.

## The edges that remain.

Late-season MLB edges concentrate in three areas.

### Specific matchup quirks.

A starter who has historically struggled against a specific lineup configuration. A bullpen that breaks down in a specific innings split. A park factor that the market underweights for a specific weather pattern. These remain priceable because they require composing multiple inputs that the market handles individually but not in combination.

### Roster turnover.

Trade deadline acquisitions, August call-ups, and injury returns introduce new information that the market has to repeatedly reprice. The first three to five games of a meaningful roster change are often where the model has its largest projected edge over the market.

### Late-season motivation gaps.

A team out of contention behaves differently than a team chasing a wild card spot. The market prices this, but the magnitude of the adjustment is often imprecise. A model that explicitly weights motivation context can find spots where the market has either over- or under-corrected.

## What this means for signal volume.

Signal counts in MLB tend to drop in late summer compared to May and June. This is not because the model is tired or because there are fewer games. It is because fewer of those games carry edges that clear the discipline filter.

<div class="observation">
  <div class="observation-eyebrow">Observation</div>
  <div class="observation-text">In the 2025 MLB season, the model fired roughly <span class="stat">1.4</span> signals per day in May and June. By August, the rate dropped to <span class="stat">0.8</span> per day. The signals that did fire had higher average closing line value, not lower.</div>
</div>

## How we use it.

The MLB model's edge threshold is constant across the season. What changes is how many games clear it. The expectation is fewer signals as the season matures, with each signal carrying more weight when it does appear.

If you see the signal count drop in August, that is not the model giving up. That is the model holding its standard against a market that has gotten smarter alongside it.
