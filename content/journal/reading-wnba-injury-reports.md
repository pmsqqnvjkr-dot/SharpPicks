# Reading WNBA injury reports: three signals the market reacts to late.

NBA injury news moves lines within minutes. A starter ruled out at 5 PM ET is reflected in the spread by 5:03. The market is built around watching the wire.

The WNBA market is slower. Not because anyone is asleep. Because the information itself moves slower, comes from fewer sources, and gets relayed by fewer reporters. That delay creates a small but real window where a careful read of the injury report produces an edge before the line moves.

Three patterns recur.

## Pattern one: the questionable starter who plays.

A WNBA starter listed as questionable plays roughly two-thirds of the time. The market shades the line as if the probability is closer to a coin flip. When the player is announced as available 30 to 60 minutes before tip, the line usually moves a half point or a full point in their team's favor.

If you can read the report carefully enough to understand which questionable designations actually mean questionable and which are precautionary, you have an edge on the half point that arrives at gametime.

The model does not try to outguess injury reports. It reads them, checks the team's recent pattern of designating that specific player, and adjusts the projection accordingly. The market often does not.

## Pattern two: the rotation player ruled out.

Star injuries get priced. Rotation injuries usually do not. A WNBA team that loses its eighth-best player has lost more than a comparable NBA team in the same situation, because the WNBA roster is shorter and the rotation runs tighter. The replacement minutes go to a player who would not normally see the floor in this game.

The market often shrugs off these injuries. The model treats them as meaningful, especially when the lost player is a defensive specialist or a designated three-point shooter. The opponent's offensive efficiency moves measurably in those situations.

## Pattern three: the unannounced minutes restriction.

This one is the hardest to catch and the most rewarding when you do. A player returning from injury is officially available, listed without restriction, and starts the game. Twenty minutes in, they are on the bench. They finish with 18 minutes played instead of their normal 30.

These minutes restrictions are almost never announced in advance. They become visible only by tracking team patterns. A coach who has historically eased players back over three to four games is likely to do it again. The market prices the player as available. The model prices them at 60 to 70 percent of their normal load.

  <div class="observation-eyebrow">Observation</div>
  <div class="observation-text">Across the 2025 season, games where a returning starter played fewer than 22 minutes saw their team underperform the spread by an average of <span class="stat negative">-2.1</span> points. The market priced these games as if the player would log a normal load.</div>
</div>

## How we use it.

The WNBA model pulls injury reports from multiple sources three times before each game. Each player carries a probability of playing, an expected minutes load, and a flag for likely-restricted return. When the model fires a signal, the injury context is one of the inputs that has already been weighted in.

What the model cannot do is replace your own attention. If you see a designation that does not match the team's typical pattern, that is a real piece of information. The model uses it. So can you.
