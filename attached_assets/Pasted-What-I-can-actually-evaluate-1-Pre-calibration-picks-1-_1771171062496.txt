This is the critical finding. Both post-cal picks hit the 10% cap, meaning the model wanted to show 47.5% and 33% edges. Even after 30/70 shrinkage, your model is still producing edges that need capping. Markets set 20+ point spreads very carefully because the public attention is high. Your model probably doesn’t have enough training data on extreme spreads to be accurate there.
3. Specific problems I can identify:
No closing line data. This is the single most important thing you’re missing. I mentioned it before — CLV is the best predictor of long-term profitability. You need to capture the closing line for every pick. If you pick Lakers -7.5 and it closes at -8.5, the market moved toward you, confirming your edge was real. If it closes at -6.5, the market moved away, suggesting your edge was noise. Start capturing this immediately. Add a job that records the line 5 minutes before tip-off.
Edge distribution is bimodal. Your pre-cal picks had edges of 4-8%. Your post-cal picks have edges so large they hit the 10% cap. There’s nothing in the middle. A well-calibrated model should produce a smooth distribution of edges, with most picks in the 3.5-6% range and rare picks above 8%. If everything either barely qualifies or smashes through the cap, the edge calculation has a scaling problem.
No predicted margin on pre-cal picks. This means you can’t retroactively evaluate whether calibration would have changed those picks. Some of those 6 wins might not have qualified under the new model. You should run the current calibrated model against those 7 games retroactively — feed in the same inputs and see what edges it produces now. If the calibrated model would have passed on 3 of those games and you still went 4-1 on the remaining ones, the calibration is working. If it would have passed on all the winners, something’s wrong.
