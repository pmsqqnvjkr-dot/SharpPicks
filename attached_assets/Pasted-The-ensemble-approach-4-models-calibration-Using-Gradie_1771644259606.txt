The ensemble approach (4 models + calibration)
Using Gradient Boosting, Random Forest, XGBoost, and AdaBoost together is solid — these are all tree-based methods though, which means they share similar inductive biases and failure modes. You’re not getting true diversity the way you would if one of them were a neural net or a linear model. When all four models are wrong, they’re likely wrong in the same direction (e.g., all overconfident on certain spread ranges). Consider whether a simple logistic regression as a 5th member would catch cases where the tree ensemble is collectively overfit.
Probability calibration is the right call and often skipped — good that it’s there.
56 features
This is a lot for the number of NBA games in a season (~1,230). Even with several seasons of training data you’re likely in the range of 3,000-8,000 training samples. With 56 features and 4 models, overfitting risk is real. The key question is how much of your feature engineering leaks future information — things like season-to-date records, rolling ratings, and book consensus odds are all fine, but injury data and line movement are particularly tricky to get right without subtle lookahead.
The walk-forward validation is the right antidote here and it’s good that it exists. The critical detail is whether each fold’s test set is truly out-of-sample on all 56 features — including the ones derived from book consensus, which can implicitly encode the outcome.
Margin-first → cover probability via Normal CDF
This is an elegant and principled approach. The key sensitivity is your calibrated sigma — if it’s estimated from training data, it needs to be re-estimated in each walk-forward fold to avoid leakage. A sigma that’s slightly too tight makes your cover probabilities look more extreme than they are, inflating apparent edge.
Market shrinkage (MODEL_WEIGHT = 0.3)
0.3 is actually quite humble — you’re saying your model contributes 30% of the final probability and the market contributes 70%. This is intellectually honest and probably right for a model that’s been live for a limited time. The risk here is that this blend can mask bad model performance: if your model is systematically wrong in one direction, the market’s 70% weight will often drag the output toward the right answer anyway, making your model look better than it is in backtests.
Worth tracking model-only accuracy vs blended accuracy separately so you know how much the market is doing the heavy lifting.
Risk filter cascade
The SFS, injury gates, rest penalties, and line movement stops are all conceptually sound. The concern is that each filter represents a degree of freedom that was presumably tuned on historical data — and each one you add increases the chance you’re fitting to noise. The All-Star break penalty (3% reduction) and the 0.3% per rest day numbers feel precise enough that I’d want to see the sample size behind them. If the All-Star break effect was observed over 5-6 seasons with maybe 20-30 affected games, that’s a noisy estimate.
The hard stop on extreme line moves (2.5+ pts against) is the one I’d trust most — that’s a genuine signal that the market knows something you don’t.
Dynamic edge threshold (3.0% + Spread × 0.167, capped at 7.0%)
This is smart — requiring a higher edge on larger spreads makes sense because large spreads have more variance and the market is generally more efficient at pricing them. The specific formula though (3.0 + spread × 0.167) is another precise-looking number that needs a real sample to justify. If this was derived by optimizing historical ROI you’ve added another layer of overfitting on top of everything else.
The edge cap at 8.0%
This one I’d push back on. Capping edge at 8% prevents you from sizing large on your best opportunities. If a game genuinely has 12% edge, you want to know that — both for sizing and for tracking whether those high-edge calls actually perform better over time. The cap makes sense as a sanity check (anything >8% is suspicious and worth a manual look) but silently clipping the number loses information.
pretip_revalidate() — revokes if edge drops below 1.5% or line moves 2+ pts
The 1.5% threshold is below your 3.5% publishing threshold, which means a pick can survive pre-tip validation even after losing most of its edge. If you published at 7.6% and it’s now at 2%, that’s a very different bet than when you published it. You might consider revoking anything that drops below 50% of its original published edge rather than a fixed floor.
The three generated explanation bullets
This is where I’d be most careful from a product/trust perspective. The bullets are generated from a template, not derived from what actually drove the model’s prediction. If the model gave a high probability because of pace/matchup data, but the explanation bullet leads with rest advantage (because rest was in the data and follows the template), users are reading a rationalization, not an explanation. Over time if your explanations and outcomes are misaligned — even subtly — it will erode trust when sophisticated users notice.
