What CLV actually tells you.

Feb 25, 2026
By Evan Cole . Head of Signal Intelligence

Most bettors judge a pick by whether it won or lost. Sharps judge it by something more important: where the line moved after they bet.

Closing line value: what it is.

When you place a bet, the market is still open. Books continue taking action, adjusting their lines based on where the sharp money flows. The line that exists at game time, called the closing line, reflects the most efficient and information-rich price the market has to offer.

If you bet a team at -3 and the line closes at -4.5, you got the better of the market. That is closing line value. You beat the closing number by a full point and a half. The outcome of the game is almost irrelevant.

Why outcomes are noise.

Single-game outcomes have huge variance. A +EV bet can lose. A -EV bet can win. Over hundreds of bets, results converge on expected value, but in any small sample, the scoreboard is mostly noise.

Closing line value is signal. If you consistently bet numbers that the market later moves in your direction, you are finding edges before the market prices them in. That is not luck. That is the definition of an information advantage.

Sharp Principle
If you consistently get the better of the closing number, the wins will follow. Focus on the process, not the outcome.

How we use it.

Every pick SharpPicks publishes is logged against its closing line. It is one of the primary ways we validate that the model is doing what it is supposed to do. Finding real edges, not manufactured ones. A model that beats closing lines at scale is a model that works. Everything else is storytelling.

The scoreboard matters. But CLV is how we know whether we deserve the score.

A note on sample size.

CLV becomes meaningful around 100 bets. Below that, you are still in noise territory. Above that, the signal starts to emerge. SharpPicks publishes its CLV record openly so you can verify the math yourself.

Observation
A bettor with +2.0 average CLV across 200 bets is almost certainly profitable long-term, even if their actual P&L is negative on that sample.
Why this matters
Understanding how the system works builds the trust needed to follow it through variance. CLV is the proof, not the picks themselves.
